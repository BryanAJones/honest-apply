# Writing Style Defaults

Universal anti-slop and anti-AI-tells rules. The main skill applies these on every draft,
regardless of the user's personal voice patterns. Personal patterns from `career-profile.md`
section 2 layer on top.

---

## Universal voice patterns — do these

- **Short sentences for emphasis.** After a complex setup, land the point in 6 words or fewer.
- **Open with a specific hook.** A pointed observation, a question, or a concrete scene.
  Never a generic throat-clearing sentence.
- **Conviction with precise hedging.** State what you don't know directly, then state what
  you do know without softening it.
- **Concrete specifics over abstraction.** Name the actual thing built, the actual metric, the
  actual decision. Not "drove improvements." What improvement, by how much?
- **Honest gap acknowledgment, unhedged.** State the gap directly — don't dress it up — then
  pivot immediately to what makes the application worth reading anyway.
- **Sentence variety.** Alternate short punchy sentences with longer compound ones. Never
  three long sentences in a row.

## Universal voice patterns — never do these

- Generic openers: "I am excited to apply…", "With my background in…"
- Three consecutive long sentences — always break with a short one
- Bullet points inside cover letters — prose only
- Corporate hedging: "results-driven," "passionate about," "proven track record,"
  "fast-paced environment," "wear many hats," "self-starter," "team player," "hit the
  ground running"
- Inflated claims that don't survive "can I defend this in an interview?"
- Ending with a summary — close with something direct or forward-looking, not a restatement

## Rhythm test

Read the cover letter aloud. If you can't find a sentence under 10 words in every other
paragraph, rewrite. If three sentences in a row have the same structure, break the pattern.

## Copy-paste survival test (~20% threshold)

If more than ~20% of sentences would survive copy-pasted into a different company's
application, rewrite the specifics until they can't.

## Anti-AI structural tells — never do these

Surface patterns, not word-level. The tells are in the *shape* of the writing, not the
vocabulary.

- **"It's not X, it's Y" / negation structure.** Sounds profound, means nothing. Never.
- **Em dash confetti.** One em dash per cover letter, max. Every additional em dash is a
  tell. Use a period or a conjunction.
- **The corporate tricolon.** Three adjectives that sound like a mission slide ("clear,
  concise, and compelling"). If three things appear in a list, they shouldn't rhyme or have
  identical cadence.
- **Generic transition glue.** "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In conclusion,"
  "It's important to note." Max one explicit transition word per paragraph.
- **Setup -> "The answer lies in..." -> vague payoff.** Rhetorical question followed by a
  generic explanation. If the structure is "Here's a tension... here's the resolution," the
  resolution must be a specific defensible fact, not a principle.
- **Paragraph summary sentences.** Ending a paragraph by restating what it just said,
  especially "Whether X or Y..." or "Ultimately..." — delete it.
- **Uniform paragraph length.** Three paragraphs of exactly four sentences each. Humans
  pause, accelerate, digress. Vary the shape deliberately.
- **Everything turned up to 11.** "Exciting opportunities," "powerful solutions,"
  "groundbreaking insights." If the adjective would fit on a LinkedIn banner, cut it.
- **Passive constructions hiding the actor.** "A decision was made," "mistakes were
  identified." Name who decided.
- **Hedging stacked on hedging.** "This may potentially help in many situations." One
  qualifier per claim, max. Prefer conditions over hedges: "This works when X" beats "This
  might work."
